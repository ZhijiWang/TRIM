## Primary Abstract: DH / CLS Methods Target

Interpretive annotation often records a final category while leaving the evidential and inferential route to that category in prose notes, adjudication discussion, or nowhere at all. This creates a methodological problem for humanistic coding: final-label agreement can conceal distinct interpretive pathways, while final-label disagreement can arise from different causes, including coder error, codebook ambiguity, insufficient evidence, compatible difference, and defensible pathway variation. This article introduces Threshold-Rationale Interpretive Modelling (TRIM), a structured annotation method that records selected primary evidence, contextual evidence, analytic anchor, function label, friction locus, rationale mechanism, epistemic support, discourse level, temporal orientation, uncertainty, and complete alternative signatures. TRIM is designed as a middle layer between categorical annotation and unrestricted interpretive memoing: it does not automate interpretation, but makes the pathway from evidence to function locatable, comparable, and reviewable. A ten-case v0.2.0 usability pilot showed independent completion but exposed instability in evidence selection, function/actor-uptake boundaries, discourse level, uncertainty calibration, question logging, shared context, and translation-mediated coding. The article reports those diagnostic findings, explains the pilot-informed v0.2.1 revisions, and presents a prospective out-of-sample retest design. The contribution is methodological rather than reliability-validating: TRIM provides a pathway architecture and audit workflow for interpretive annotation, while final claims about intercoder reliability, cross-language validity, and domain generality require the completed retest and larger future studies.

## Alternative Abstract: Technical / Software Target

TRIM (Threshold-Rationale Interpretive Modelling) is a lightweight Python-supported workflow for reviewable interpretive annotation. Instead of storing only final labels, TRIM represents each annotation as an evidence-to-function pathway consisting of evidence nodes, source-facing anchor, analytic anchor, controlled threshold-rationale fields, uncertainty, rationale note, and optional complete alternative signature. The software layer provides schema objects, controlled-vocabulary validation, signature parsing, graph export, field-level intercoder comparison, compound-value compatibility metrics, evidence-segment overlap and Jaccard measures, shared-context validation, question-log validation, and leakage-tested coder-package generation. A locked ten-case v0.2.0 usability pilot demonstrated independent completion and identified specific failure points in evidence selection, function boundaries, discourse level, uncertainty, language access, and question logging. The current v0.2.1 package responds with discriminative primary/context evidence fields, explicit language-access and shared-context metadata, revised manuals, a 12-case out-of-sample retest corpus, and deterministic package checks. TRIM should not be understood as an automated interpretation system or as validated reliability evidence. Its software contribution is infrastructure for preserving, checking, comparing, and revisiting human interpretive pathways. A formal release should accompany publication, with retest outcomes reported separately or as an empirical extension.
