# v0.2.0 Usability Pilot Results and Adjudication

## Status

The v0.2.0 ten-case pilot is a locked historical record. Coder A and Coder B
completed independent annotations before adjudication. Adjudicator 1 reviewed
the locked submissions and the post-pilot interview. No original coder values
are overwritten by this report.

The pilot is best described as a multilingual, translation- and
summary-mediated usability pilot. It is not evidence of established
population-level reliability or cross-language construct validity.

## Completion

- Ten formal cases were completed.
- No required annotation fields were missing in the locked coding submission.
- The question log was initially empty.
- The post-pilot interview clarified that questions did arise but were resolved
  internally because the guide implied that only blocking questions or protocol
  deviations belonged in the log.

Compound and single-select differences were not collapsed into one score.

where the difference reflected dominance ordering rather than incompatible
interpretation.

## Adjudicated Categories

Adjudication distinguished:

- substantive pathway variation;
- codebook ambiguity;
- compatible difference;
- near-complete alignment;
- insufficient evidence;
- function-boundary ambiguity.

Two cases were retained as substantive pathway-variation cases. In those cases,
both complete pathways were textually plausible and should inform future
boundary examples rather than be reduced to coder error.

## Main Findings

### Function Boundary

The pilot exposed confusion between the analytic/narrative function assigned by
the research project and what an actor does with evidence inside the case.

The revision therefore clarifies that actor uptake counts as the function only
when action-guiding standing is the central analytic role. A character acting on
a sign does not automatically create authorization.

### Evidence Selection

Coder B selected all supplied segments in every formal case. The adjudication
and interview indicate that fixed case boundaries and broad instructions made
all-segment selection feel safer than discriminative selection.

v0.2.1 therefore requires one to three primary evidence segments and separates
contextual support.

### `context_inference`

One disagreement showed that `context_inference` could be selected even when a
textual actor visibly performed the interpretive bridge. v0.2.1 adds an
explicit-textual-operation exclusion rule.

### Warrant Boundaries

At least one prophecy-confirmation case retained legitimate variation between
`warrant_attribution` and `warrant_relation`. The categories are not collapsed.
v0.2.1 documents when one source gains standing and when interaction among
confirmed and unconfirmed warrants carries the function.

### Operation and Perspective

A confession/testimony case retained a meaningful alternative between
confession as an operation and speaker standpoint as the dominant pathway.
v0.2.1 keeps both as possible legitimate alternatives.

### Discourse Level

The three testimony cases showed systematic divergence. One pathway coded the
local speech act; the other coded the larger textual frame. v0.2.1 specifies
that coders should choose the level at which the conversion is analytically
operative and record the non-dominant level when relevant.

### Uncertainty

Coder B used low uncertainty more often than the adjudication supported. The
post-pilot interview confirmed that a complete alternative pathway should have
triggered medium uncertainty more often. v0.2.1 revises the thresholds.

### Question Log

The empty log did not mean no questions arose. v0.2.1 requires every
definitional, interpretive, procedural, or packet-level question to be logged,
including self-resolved questions.

### Language And Translation

The interview confirmed direct English reading, but substantial reliance on
close support, segment maps, linked full text, and summaries for Classical
Chinese and Japanese materials. Summaries sometimes increased certainty and
highlighted what mattered. The pilot should not be described as original-text
cross-language reproducibility.

### Shared Narrative Context

One testimony case lacked explicit permission to use cross-testimony relations
even though one analytic pathway depended on accumulated incompatibility. v0.2.1
therefore adds explicit case scope and shared-context metadata.

### Corpus-To-Function Prior Risk

The interview reported that source family made some labels feel obvious before
close analysis. v0.2.1 uses an out-of-sample corpus with distractor cases and
avoids one-to-one source/function mapping.

## Claims Not Supported

This pilot does not establish:

- validated intercoder reliability;
- universal reproducibility;
- cross-language construct validity;
- superiority over free-text memos;
- domain-general stability.

It supports a narrower claim: the first independent usability pilot identified
specific manual, packet, evidence-selection, uncertainty, language-access, and
shared-context revisions that should be retested prospectively on new cases.
