# TRIM-HAA Participant Function-Label Guide

Status: template plus synthetic demonstration. The final study label set is not frozen in this package.

Ethics-submission blocker: final ethics submission requires the actual participant-facing function-label guide for the selected cases. This demonstration guide is not study material for data collection.

Expected session length: approximately 60-90 minutes, including instructions, practice, breaks, annotation tasks, and feedback questions. This duration range is provisional and must be finalised before ethics submission and recruitment.

## How To Use This Guide

A function label is the category that best describes the role or function you identify in the case. Unless the final study guide says otherwise, choose one label only.

If two labels seem plausible, choose the one best supported by your selected primary evidence. Use uncertainty or an alternative pathway when another interpretation remains defensible.

Whether labels are mutually exclusive: final study decision required.

Whether one label only is permitted: final study decision required. Pilot default is one label only.

Whether a no-fit option is available: final study decision required. If available, use it when none of the labels adequately fits.

## Synthetic Demonstration Label Set

The labels below are examples for explaining the task structure only.

### Label: Caution

Plain-language definition: The text shows a character or speaker avoiding risk or checking before acting.

Synthetic positive example: "Mira checked the hallway twice before opening the door."

Synthetic near-miss or contrast example: "Mira opened the door quickly because she was excited." This may show eagerness rather than caution.

Boundary notes: Caution requires evidence of risk awareness or careful checking, not simply slow movement.

### Label: Suspicion

Plain-language definition: The text shows a character or speaker doubting someone or expecting hidden motives.

Synthetic positive example: "Mira hid the letter because she thought Jonah had read the last one."

Synthetic near-miss or contrast example: "Mira forgot where she put the letter." This may show forgetfulness rather than suspicion.

Boundary notes: Suspicion requires evidence of distrust or suspected concealment.

### Label: Reassurance

Plain-language definition: The text shows someone trying to reduce fear, doubt, or tension.

Synthetic positive example: "Jonah said the windows were locked and stayed until Mira smiled."

Synthetic near-miss or contrast example: "Jonah changed the topic." This may avoid tension without clearly reducing it.

Boundary notes: Reassurance needs evidence that the action or speech is meant to make someone feel more secure.

### Label: No Fit

Plain-language definition: None of the available labels adequately describes the case.

Synthetic positive example: A passage only describes the weather and has no evidence of caution, suspicion, or reassurance.

Synthetic near-miss or contrast example: A passage with weak evidence of caution should usually be marked uncertain rather than no fit.

Boundary notes: Use no fit only when the available labels do not work, not simply because the case is difficult.
